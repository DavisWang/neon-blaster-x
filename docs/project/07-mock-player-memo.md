## First-Session Verdict

- Would I keep playing for another five minutes? Yes.
- Why or why not? The game now reads like a complete small-form arcade survival build: the salvage fantasy is legible quickly, the enemy roster has recognizable silhouettes, and the added audio gives the combat loop a stronger sense of place without turning noisy.

## What Feels Good

- The title screen establishes the tone immediately.
- The builder palette makes the block language understandable before the player even fights.
- The centered-ship gameplay presentation reads like a focused arcade prototype instead of an overbuilt sandbox.
- The melodic blaster notes and restrained ambient loop support the neon tone without fighting the visuals.
- The current roster and AI spread suggest more replay value than the earlier shell-only versions.

## What Feels Bad Or Unfair

- Some long-run balance and salvage-under-pressure feel could still be tuned further.
- The builder-only rainbow test seed is useful for development, but it is clearly a dev convenience rather than a diegetic progression feature.

## Control And Responsiveness

- The shipped keyboard-and-mouse model is coherent: movement, firing, dragging, detaching, and rotation all fit the game's arcade-builder loop.

## Challenge And Balance

- The current direction looks appropriate for a short arcade survival loop.
- The main future tuning risk is pacing, not feature incompleteness.

## Quick Wins

- Tighten the remaining shell wording and release docs so the repo tells the same story as the game.
- Keep watching early salvage readability and the first-pressure curve in future balancing passes.

## Bigger Bets

- Let better-quality enemy ships telegraph their loadout even more clearly before the fight so players can hunt upgrades intentionally.
- Consider chunkier salvage or scripted encounters later if the current arcade loop needs a second layer of spectacle.
